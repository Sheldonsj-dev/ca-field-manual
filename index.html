The Big Picture — What Is This Job, Really?
🏥
Think of yourself as the hospital's deal-maker and deal-keeper
Every time the hospital needs something from an outside company — a new MRI machine, a cleaning service, a software system, a staffing agency — someone has to write up the agreement that protects the hospital. That's you. You make sure every deal is fair, legal, clearly written, and then followed through on. When a vendor doesn't do what they promised, you're the one who holds them accountable.
Real-world analogy: Think about buying a house. There's a contract that says the seller will fix the roof before closing. The contract administrator is the person who makes sure that contract says exactly the right thing, gets signed by the right people, and that the roof actually gets fixed — or takes action if it doesn't.
⚖️
Why does this role exist in a hospital specifically?
Hospitals are unlike most businesses. They receive federal money (Medicare and Medicaid), they handle people's private health information, and they're held to strict safety and quality standards. One poorly written contract — or a missing clause — can expose the hospital to millions of dollars in fines, lawsuits, or even loss of its Medicare funding. This role exists to prevent that from ever happening.
Real-world analogy: A regular business might sign a simple deal with a cleaning company on a handshake. A hospital can't do that — there are federal laws that require the cleaning company to protect patient privacy, follow safety protocols, and prove they're insured. Your job is to make all of that happen in writing.
📋
What does a typical day look like?
No two days are exactly alike, but you'll generally be doing one or more of these things: reviewing a contract a vendor sent over and marking up the parts that need to change; helping a department manager put into words exactly what they need from a supplier; negotiating terms with a vendor's legal team; routing a contract through the approval process; tracking which contracts are about to expire; or auditing whether vendors are actually doing what their contracts say.
💡 Remember: You are not working alone. You have legal counsel, finance, compliance, and your Supply Chain director all backing you up. Your job is to coordinate and drive — not to know everything yourself from day one.

Core Responsibilities — Plain English
📝
Making Sure Every Deal Has a Contract
You are responsible for making sure that every time the hospital buys something or hires a company to do something, there is a written contract in place before work starts. No verbal agreements, no handshakes, no "we'll figure it out." Everything in writing.
Think of it like this: Before a contractor comes to renovate your kitchen, you'd want a written contract that says exactly what they'll do, when they'll finish, how much it costs, and what happens if they do it wrong. You do the same thing — but for a $300M portfolio of deals.
🔍
Reading and Analyzing Contracts
Vendors often send their own contracts written to favor themselves. Your job is to read those contracts carefully, spot anything that's unfair or legally risky for the hospital, and flag or change those terms before anyone signs. You look for things like: who is responsible if something goes wrong? What does the vendor promise to deliver? What happens if they don't? Can the hospital get out of the deal if needed?
Think of it like this: Like reading the fine print before you sign a phone contract — except the stakes are millions of dollars and federal compliance, not a two-year phone plan.
🤝
Negotiating
Contracts are not take-it-or-leave-it. You negotiate. That means going back to the vendor and saying "we need this clause changed" or "that price isn't acceptable" or "we need a 30-day out clause in case this doesn't work." You prepare a strategy before every negotiation, know what the hospital's must-haves are, and push for the best deal possible.
Think of it like this: Buying a car. The sticker price isn't the final price. You know what you want, what you'll pay, and what you'll walk away from. That's negotiation — and you do it for contracts worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.
📬
Routing for Approval
Once a contract is drafted and negotiated, it can't just be signed by anyone. Depending on the dollar value and risk level, it has to go through specific people for review and approval — legal, finance, a VP, maybe the CEO. You manage this process, make sure the right eyes see it, and nothing slips through without proper sign-off.
Think of it like this: A big purchase at a company has to go through a chain of approvals. Under $500? A manager can approve it. Over $50,000? It needs the CFO. You know the exact rules and make sure every contract follows them.
📊
Managing the Portfolio
Once contracts are signed, they don't disappear. You track every active contract in a contract management system — when it expires, when it needs to be renewed, whether the vendor is delivering what they promised. Think of it like managing a filing system, except the files are worth hundreds of millions of dollars and missing a renewal deadline can mean the hospital loses a critical service overnight.
💡 Key habit to build: Set calendar reminders 90, 60, and 30 days before every contract expires. Never let a contract lapse without a decision to renew, renegotiate, or end it.
🔎
Auditing and Compliance Checks
Periodically, you review active contracts to make sure both sides are doing what they promised. Is the vendor hitting their service levels? Is the hospital fulfilling its payment obligations? Are there any regulatory changes that now affect the terms? This is how you catch problems early before they become expensive disputes.
90-Day Plan

Your first three months, week by week.

You won't be expected to know everything on day one. Here's the path from "I just started" to "I've got this."

The golden rule of your first 90 days: Ask questions before you act, and document everything. There's no such thing as a stupid question here — a wrong assumption on a $500,000 contract is far more costly than looking like a beginner.
Phase 1 — Days 1 to 14: Understand the World You're In
1
Week 1
Learn the organization
Before you can protect the hospital through contracts, you need to understand how it works. Who are the key people? How does Supply Chain fit in? What kinds of things does the hospital buy and from whom?
  • Meet your Supply Chain director and key team members
  • Tour the departments you'll be contracting for
  • Review the organizational chart — understand who reports to whom
  • Learn the $300M+ contract portfolio at a high level — what categories exist?
  • Walk through the contract management system (CMS) with your supervisor
2
Week 2
Shadow and observe real contracts
The best way to learn contracts is to see them in action. Follow a senior administrator through at least 3 live contracts from start to finish. Take notes. Ask "why" at every step.
  • Shadow a contract from RFP through execution
  • Read 5 executed contracts from different categories
  • Learn the difference between an agreement and a contract
  • Review the delegation of authority (DOA) policy — who can approve what
Phase 2 — Days 15 to 45: Learn the Mechanics
3
Week 3
How to read and analyze a contract
Learn the language. Practice spotting red flags — the clauses that put the hospital at risk — in real anonymized contracts.
  • Learn the standard sections of a contract and what each one covers
  • Practice markup on 3 real anonymized contracts using the red flag checklist
  • Meet with General Counsel for a 1-hour compliance overview
4
Week 4
Drafting — writing your first contracts
You'll learn to use the hospital's contract templates and understand when a custom clause is needed. You'll draft your first Statement of Work.
  • Deep dive into the template library — what exists and when to use each
  • Draft a Statement of Work (SOW) for a real or hypothetical scenario
  • Peer review with senior admin — get feedback
5
Week 5
Procurement — how to find and select vendors
Learn the RFI, RFQ, and RFP process — the formal ways the hospital solicits and evaluates vendors before signing a deal.
  • Understand when to use RFI vs. RFQ vs. RFP
  • Run a mock RFP from start to finish
  • Learn risk assessment and contract type selection
6
Week 6
Negotiation — your most powerful skill
Practice negotiation through role-play scenarios. Learn how to handle pushback, protect must-have terms, and build relationships with vendors while still holding firm.
  • Learn the hospital's negotiation framework and strategy approach
  • Role-play 3 supplier pushback scenarios with your trainer
  • Debrief after each — what worked, what would you change?
Phase 3 — Days 46 to 75: Do Real Work With Support
7
Weeks 7 & 8
Your first real contracts
You'll be assigned 2-3 real contracts to manage from start to finish — with your supervisor reviewing each step. You make the decisions; they coach you through them.
8
Weeks 9 & 10
Stakeholder management
Start building your relationships — meet with department managers, directors, and vendor account managers. Learn how to manage competing priorities and resolve conflicts professionally.
9
Week 11
Your first compliance audit
Review 5 active contracts against their terms. Are vendors performing? Are there any regulatory gaps? Present your findings to your supervisor.
Phase 4 — Days 76 to 90: Own It
10
Weeks 12 & 13
Full independence
By now, you manage your portfolio independently. Your supervisor is a resource, not a reviewer. You complete your 90-day self-assessment, set your year-one goals, and present your personal contract playbook — a reference binder you've built throughout training.
End-of-Day Habit — Your Daily 5-Minute Check
  • Are there any contracts expiring in the next 90 days that need action?
  • Did I enter all today's activity into the contract management system?
  • Are there any outstanding approvals I need to follow up on?
  • Did I respond to all internal customer and vendor requests within 24 hours?
  • Is anything I'm working on creating risk for the hospital that I haven't flagged to my supervisor?

Rules & Laws

Where all the rules come from — and why they matter to you.

Healthcare contracting has more rules than almost any other industry. Here's why — and where every rule originates. Think of it as five layers, each one adding more obligations.

The key insight: You don't need to be a lawyer. But you need to know enough to recognize when something in a contract might violate one of these rules — and know to call Legal before you proceed. When in doubt, escalate.
🏛️
The 5-Layer Stack — How Rules Are Built
Think of the regulatory system like a stack of filters. Every contract has to pass through all five layers before it's truly compliant:
① U.S. Federal Laws — Congress passes these. They apply everywhere.
② Federal Agency Rules — Government agencies fill in the details.
③ Accreditation Standards — The Joint Commission sets additional requirements.
④ Virginia State Laws — State-level rules layered on top of federal.
⑤ MWHC Internal Policy — The hospital's own rules that govern your daily workflow.

Which solicitation type do I use?
Three questions to find the right procurement approach.
Do you already know exactly what you want to buy — specific product, quantity, and specifications are clear?
Are you ready to commit to a purchase now, or just gathering market information first?
Will you evaluate vendors on factors beyond just price — methodology, team, experience, or proposed solution?
📋
Use an RFP — Request for Proposal
Complex need, solution isn't pre-defined, evaluating on multiple criteria. Vendors submit full proposals. You score on best overall value — not just lowest price.

Examples: New EHR system, outsourced food service, major construction, staffing agency selection.
💲
Use an RFQ — Request for Quotation
You know exactly what you need — just want competitive pricing. Lowest qualified bid typically wins.

Examples: Specific brand gloves in known quantity, office furniture to spec, routine lab supplies.
🔍
Use an RFI — Request for Information
Early exploration — understand the market before committing to anything. No obligation to buy. Helps define requirements and identify vendors before issuing an RFQ or RFP.

Examples: Exploring whether to outsource a service, researching technology options, pre-budget planning.
Which contract type should I use?
The right type determines who bears financial risk.
Is the scope of work clearly and completely defined — you know exactly what the vendor will deliver?
Is this a one-time project or an ongoing recurring service?
Is controlling total cost certainty more important than allowing flexibility for the vendor?
🔒
Fixed-Price Contract
Vendor delivers at a set price regardless of their actual costs. Hospital risk: Low. Vendor risk: High.

Examples: Building renovation, software implementation, equipment purchase.
📦
IDIQ or Blanket Purchase Agreement
Agreed pricing and terms with individual orders placed as needed. Perfect for ongoing services where exact volume isn't known upfront. Hospital risk: Medium.

Examples: Medical supplies, office products, on-call staffing.
⏱
Time & Materials (T&M)
Pay actual hours at agreed rates plus actual materials. Used when scope can't be fully defined. Always include a not-to-exceed (NTE) cap. Hospital risk: High.

Examples: Emergency repairs, IT consulting, specialized clinical consulting.
📊
Cost-Reimbursable Contract
Reimburse vendor's actual costs plus an agreed fee. Requires strong audit rights and cost controls. Hospital risk: Highest.

Examples: Research partnerships, pilot programs, highly specialized clinical services.

Does this vendor need a BAA?
A BAA is required by HIPAA whenever a vendor may access patient information. Missing one can result in massive fines.
Will the vendor access, store, transmit, or process any patient information — including names, dates, diagnoses, appointments, billing, or any identifiable health data?
Is the vendor providing a service to MWHC (rather than providing direct patient care themselves)?
Does the vendor already have a current, signed BAA on file with MWHC?
⚠
BAA Required — Do Not Proceed Without One
This vendor must sign a BAA before accessing any patient information. Federal law — not optional.

Next steps: Notify Compliance → Use MWHC's approved BAA template → Get it signed and filed in CMS → Vendor cannot begin work until BAA is executed.
✓
BAA Already on File — Verify It's Current
Good — a BAA exists. Verify: (1) it hasn't expired, and (2) it covers the specific services in this new contract. If scope has changed, an updated BAA may be needed. Check with Compliance if unsure.
✓
BAA Not Required for This Vendor
If the vendor truly has zero access to patient information, a BAA is not required. However — many vendors that seem unrelated to patient care touch PHI indirectly. When in doubt, ask Compliance before concluding no BAA is needed.
ℹ
Different Rules Apply — Consult Legal
Vendors who are themselves covered entities (another hospital, physician group, health plan) have different HIPAA obligations. The contract still needs specific privacy language. Have Legal review the data handling provisions before execution.
Vendor isn't doing what they promised — what do I do?
Follow these steps in order. The sequence matters legally — never skip steps.
1
Document the failure — in writing, immediately
Write exactly what the vendor was supposed to do, what they did (or didn't do), and the date. Pull the contract and confirm their obligation. Email your supervisor the same day.
📋 Reference: Service Level Agreement (SLA) or Statement of Work (SOW)
2
Notify the vendor in writing
Send a formal written notice to the vendor's account manager AND their leadership. Be factual — state the specific obligation, the specific failure, and the date. No threats yet. CC your Supply Chain Director. This starts the cure period clock.
3
Check the cure period in the contract
Most contracts give the vendor 10–30 days to fix the problem. Look for the "Default," "Remedy," or "Cure" clause. Mark the deadline on your calendar immediately.
4
Monitor — did they fix it?
Track whether the vendor takes corrective action. Document every communication. If they fix it within the cure period, confirm in writing that the matter is resolved and file everything in the CMS.
5A
Fixed → Close the issue
Document the cure. File in CMS. Consider whether a contract amendment is needed to add stronger performance guarantees going forward.
5B
NOT fixed → Escalate immediately
Notify Legal and Supply Chain Director. Options: liquidated damages, termination for cause, dispute resolution.

⚠ Never verbally tell a vendor they are terminated. All termination must be in writing, follow the contract's notice requirements exactly, and be approved by Legal first.
